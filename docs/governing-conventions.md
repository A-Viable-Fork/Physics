# Governing conventions

This document carries three policies the whole grading structure inherits from Stage 0 forward. None of them is re-decided by a later stage; a later stage cites this document rather than re-litigating it. Each policy is traced to the recon report section that argued for it.

## 1. Checker identity

The pipeline's checkers are AI systems the operator orchestrates: Gemini DR, DeepSeek, ChatGPT arbitration, and Claude itself under the operator's session. The protocol's `independence` field on a checking record (`distinct-party` or `self`) is a declared value the gate trusts structurally (protocol spec Section 2.3); whether one orchestrated system is a distinct party from another, or from the operator, is a semantic-attenuation question the protocol explicitly leaves to the community layer (protocol spec Section 1, "whether a named checker exists, whether claimed independence is real... is out of scope and community-level"). This kernel is that community layer's standing answer:

- **Each external AI system is its own `checker_id`, with its own source footprint.** `checker:gemini-dr`, `checker:deepseek`, `checker:chatgpt-arb`, extendable as new systems enter the pipeline. A checker's footprint traces to the sources that inform it (the report it read, the corpus it was pointed at), never shared wholesale with another checker's footprint merely because the operator ran both.
- **Cross-system checks declare `independence: distinct-party`.** Gemini checking a claim Claude produced, or DeepSeek checking a claim Gemini produced, is a distinct-party check: different pipeline, different failure modes, a real second look.
- **Same-pipeline checks declare `independence: self`.** Claude checking Claude's own output under the same operator session is a self check. It may still be useful (a second pass catches slips), but it does not contribute to a claim's own basis (protocol spec Section 2.3: "only a `distinct-party` check contributes to a claim's own basis"), and it does not feed the independence lift.
- **Two audits tracing to the same upstream report share a footprint and are not independent**, even if they run through different `checker_id`s. If Gemini and DeepSeek both audit the same prior Gemini report rather than the underlying primary sources, their footprints intersect at that report, and the source table's `rests_on` chains carry this: `footprintClosure` (`kernel/schema/tables.mjs`) computes the reflexive-transitive closure over `rests_on`, and the independence lift to `independently-rechecked` requires two `distinct-party` checks whose footprints do not collapse into one under that closure.
- **The operator is the semantic-attenuation layer.** The gate trusts `checker_id` and `independence` as declared fields; it does not itself adjudicate whether a declared distinct party is really distinct. That adjudication is the operator's, made once per checker relationship and then held to, not re-argued per claim. This document is the standing account of what the declarations mean, so the convention is legible policy rather than an implicit inflation of how many independent eyes actually looked.

## 2. The Lean policy: constitutive mode, not gate criterion

Lean is the constitutive mode of the existing grade lattice, not the gate's closing criterion (recon report Section 4). A derivation grounds at the settled-empirical tier through derivation-audit checking records: one distinct-party audit reaches `checked`, two with disjoint footprints reach `independently-rechecked` (Section 1). A Lean proof is the `closing_condition` on the residual gap between audited and proved; when a proof lands and compiles, a fork retypes the claim to the `theorem` kind at the constitutive floor. Settled-empirical and constitutive are incomparable modes by design (recon report Section 2 mapping table), and comparing them is a decline: an audited derivation and a formal proof are different kinds of settled, not ranks of one kind.

Making Lean the gate criterion ("grounds" = "compiles") was considered and rejected. It would floor essentially the entire corpus at `asserted`, erasing the audit-tier distinctions the trellis spent eighteen months earning, and it would invert the incentive against opening derivation claims at all. The current Lean state (compiling modules with structural placeholders, a rank-44 proof estimated at hundreds of manual lines, a decade-scale tag on the exhaust PDE) makes a compile-gate demand the corpus cannot yet meet. Formalization is pull-driven instead: every unproved derivation permanently displays its distance from the proof floor, and the characterized-gaps read is the formalization backlog, prioritized. Stage 1 does not re-decide this; it enters derivations at their audited tier and lets Lean lift them later.

## 3. The verdict-ladder policy: extensions only, never the gate

The DG trellis's six-step contract verdict ladder (Confirmed / Strongly Supported / Marginal / Suggestive / Plausible / Failed) does not become a local grade position. The earned-grade computation runs over the fixed confidence line (`kernel/schema/confidence.mjs`, `kernel/grounding/earned-grade.mjs`); the ladder is a domain judgment the gate deliberately does not adjudicate ("marginal" is a reading of how close a number sits to a threshold, not a grounding-structure fact). The compression is lossy by design and correctly so.

The verdict rides in a claim's `extensions` object: undeclared fields are canonicalized and hashed into the record (protocol spec Section 2) but read by no check. The gate never reads it; the client periphery renders it for a human reading the graph. Minting local grade names for the ladder positions, though permitted as policy, would not enter the fold the gate computes over, and this kernel does not do it. Stage 1 enters domain verdicts in `extensions` from the first claim that carries one, not as a later migration.

## 4. `peer-reviewed` is external literature only

`source_class: "peer-reviewed"` marks a document that has actually been through human review at a venue: a journal, a conference proceedings, a body with an editorial or refereeing process external to this corpus's own generation. No corpus-internal document takes this class until human review has actually occurred, regardless of what its own title claims. `corpus.peer-review` (Stage 2's "Peer Review Report: State-Dependent Vacuum Geometry") is not peer-reviewed by this definition: no document in this corpus has been seen by a human reviewer, so a document's self-description as a "peer review" does not govern its class. It is an adversarial audit of the framework in the same genre as the corpus's other AI-system audit reports and is classed `ai-audit` (Stage 3a Track 0, operator ruling 2). The test for a future entry is mechanical: is there a named external venue with an actual review process, distinct from this project's own AI-assisted generation pipeline? If yes, `peer-reviewed`. If the document is itself a pipeline product, whatever its title says, it is not.

## 5. The independence-lift policy for `rests_on` lineage groups

An `independently-rechecked` lift (two `distinct-party` checking records whose footprints do not collapse into one under `footprintClosure`) may cite, as a disjoint-footprint pair, only sources whose lineage is affirmatively known: a source in the **root** group (no earlier corpus document to rest on, confirmed by a close read) or the **parallel-independent** group (two or more investigations of the same question confirmed to proceed by different pathways with no citation of each other). Both are affirmative determinations, made by reading the text, not absence of a link.

A source whose `rests_on` lineage is **unknown** (no determinable predecessor found, listed as a gap rather than defaulted, per `docs/status-ledger.md`'s Stage 2.5 Track 3 gap list) caps any lift through it at `checked`, never `independently-rechecked`, until its lineage is resolved. Unknown is never read as independent: a footprint that has not been traced might collapse into another footprint once traced, and the gate's independence lift must not assume the favorable case merely because the unfavorable one has not been demonstrated. This is the same discipline the lineage gap list itself follows (a visible gap over a guessed link), applied one layer up to what the gap list is then permitted to certify.

Resolution of an unknown lineage group is lazy: done per pair, at the moment a lift actually needs it, never as a bulk pass over the whole gap list. A bulk pass would spend effort resolving lineage no lift will ever read, and would tempt the same guessing the gap list was built to avoid. `check-independence.mjs` (Stage 3b) mechanizes this policy against the real `rests_on` data and the real lineage groups recorded in the ledger; this document states the rule, Stage 3b implements it.
